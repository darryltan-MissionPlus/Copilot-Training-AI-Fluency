Welcome

Welcome Onboard

Welcome to this hands-on session on thinking and working with AI! This isn't just about writing better prompts. You'll learn a repeatable process for thinking and working with AI that you can use every day, with any AI tool. By the end, you'll have a practical framework and real experience applying it to your own work.

What You'll Learn

By the end of this session, you will be able to:

  • Apply a simple 4-step process (TAE-O) every time you work with AI
  • Think critically about what you need before you start typing
  • Ask effectively by providing rich context and using AI to help you craft better prompts
  • Evaluate AI outputs instead of accepting them at face value
  • Take ownership of what you use, share, or publish
  • Reflect on your AI interactions to continuously improve

Remember: This guide serves as your reference during and after the workshop. Use it to capture your prompts, ideas, and reflections as we go through each activity.

How LLMs Work
What You Need to Know About LLMs

You don't need to be a data scientist. But a few things about how LLMs work will help you understand why the skills and process in this guide matter. Click each card to learn more.

LLMs cannot read your mind

Click to flip
Why this matters

They only work with what you tell them. If your request is vague, the output will be vague. Always provide clear context, constraints, and details.

LLMs will not proactively ask questions

Click to flip
Why this matters

You need to volunteer context, constraints, and missing details instead of waiting for the tool to ask. Be proactive in sharing information.

LLMs tend to be helpful and agree

Click to flip
Why this matters

They may build on wrong premises. You can ask them to question your assumptions or play devil's advocate.

LLMs can and will hallucinate

Click to flip
Why this matters

Their answers come from patterns, not a source of truth. You are responsible for fact-checking everything they produce.

LLMs don't actually know what they are doing

Click to flip
Why this matters

They predict likely words based on patterns. You must apply your own judgement and adapt what you get to your specific situation.

Understand You and Your Co-pilot R&Rs
Who Does What?

You are the Pilot

  • Set direction and provide context
  • Give feedback and make decisions
  • Accountable for final output

Copilot is the Co-pilot

  • Does heavy lifting and drafts content
  • Generates options and alternatives
  • Follows your lead

Remember: When things go wrong, it's usually because one of you stepped outside your role. Stay in the pilot seat!

How to Talk to AI

Now that you understand how LLMs work and your role as the Pilot, let's look at three skills that will make you effective in that role.

1 Context is King

The more relevant context you provide, the better the response. Use the Goal, Context, Expectations framework:

ElementWhat to IncludeExample
GoalWhat you want the AI to produce“List the top 3 challenges...”
ContextBackground, audience, key facts“I'm an AI Champion helping...”
ExpectationsTone, format, length, must include/avoid“Keep each to 2-3 sentences...”

Pro tip: You don't need all three every time, but the more specific you are, the better your results.

Prompt Examples

Example 1: Summarizing a quarterly report for leadership
Vague Prompt

Help me summarize this report.

Structured Prompt (G-C-E)
Structured Prompt with G-C-E Labels
Goal
Summarize the Q3 performance report so I can brief the leadership team on key results and recommended actions.
Context
Your Role: Project Manager preparing a leadership briefing. Importance: The leadership team needs a clear picture of Q3 results, what's on track, and where we need to act. They have limited time. Audience: Senior leaders with varying familiarity with day-to-day operations. Source: Use only the content in the report I provide. Do not add external information.
Expectations
Output Format: 5-bullet executive summary (most important results) / Key achievements and wins / Areas of concern or risk / Recommended next steps (with owners if possible) / Open questions for leadership to discuss. Tone: Clear, professional, and concise (avoid jargon). Length: Keep under ~300 words.
Example 2: Preparing talking points for a stakeholder meeting
Vague Prompt

Help me prepare for a meeting with stakeholders.

Structured Prompt (G-C-E)
Structured Prompt with G-C-E Labels
Goal
Help me prepare talking points for a stakeholder meeting so I can present our project status clearly and address potential concerns proactively.
Context
Your Role: Team Lead presenting a project update to key stakeholders. Importance: The project is behind schedule by two weeks. I need to explain the delay, show the recovery plan, and maintain stakeholder confidence. Audience: Department Heads and the Project Sponsor in a 30-minute meeting. Source: Refer to the project status update and timeline I will provide.
Expectations
Output Format: A brief opening statement (30-45 seconds) / 5-6 key talking points with supporting data / 3 questions stakeholders are likely to ask (with suggested responses) / 2-3 proposed next steps with timeline / A closing statement that reinforces confidence. Tone: Professional, transparent but solution-focused, calm under pressure. Include alternative phrasing for sensitive points.

2 Just Ask Copilot (Meta-Prompting)

We know we need to include as much context as possible, but how do we know what we've missed? We don't know what we don't know!

Use a simple “meta-prompt” to ask Copilot to act as a coach, review your initial prompt, and tell you what crucial details are missing.

The Prompt Coach Meta-Prompt

Act as a Prompt Coach. (1) Critically review the prompt below. Note any crucial details that are missing. (2) Rewrite it for clarity and completeness, adding reasonable defaults for gaps in []. (3) Ask 2-3 follow-up questions to refine it further. Prompt: [Insert your initial prompt here, include your G-C-E]

Remember: Your role is to guide Copilot, not to do its job. Give Copilot the full context (Goal, Context, Expectations) so it can generate the best output. Don't spend time crafting the “perfect” prompt yourself; Copilot is better at that than you!

3 Keep the Conversation Going

You will not get the perfect response in the first attempt, and that's perfectly normal.

Treat the first response from Copilot as a draft, not the final product. The real value comes from the conversation. Don't stop after the first response. Review, refine, and iterate until you get what you need.

Review

Check relevance, completeness, and accuracy of the response.

Feedback

Tell Copilot what to keep, change, or remove.

Iterate

Refine and repeat until the output meets your needs.

The 4-Step Process

TAE-O is a simple, repeatable process you run every time you work with AI. Four steps, four questions. It puts together the skills you just learned into a workflow you can use every day.

A

T THINK — “What do I actually need?”

Input

Your real work task or “Prompt Away” task

Action

Define what you need before typing anything into AI

Output

A clearly scoped task with success criteria

Stop before you type. Define what you need, why it matters, and what success looks like. This is the step most people skip, and it's the one that makes the biggest difference.

The THINK Checklist

QuestionAsk Yourself
What is the task?What am I trying to accomplish?
Why does it matter?What's at stake? Who is this for?
What does success look like?How will I know if the AI output is good enough?
What context do I have?What information, constraints, or examples can I provide?
Worked Example

Scenario: You need to prepare a project status update for senior leadership.

THINK before typing:

  • Task: Summarise Q3 results for a leadership briefing
  • Why: Leadership needs a clear picture; limited time in meeting
  • Success: Concise summary with key results, risks, and actions
  • Context: I have the full Q3 report; audience is senior leaders

Notice how you haven't typed anything into AI yet. That's the point. Thinking first means you'll ask better, evaluate faster, and own the result with confidence.

Instructions: - Based on what you know about me, suggest the top 5 ways that you can help me in my context. - Keep all ideas within Copilot Chat's native capabilities (no external integrations). - For each use case, provide: Description, Expected Benefit, Ease of Implementation.

A ASK — “How do I set AI up for success?”

Input

Your scoped task and context from THINK

Action

Output

A ready-to-use prompt

Now you're ready to talk to AI. Apply the skills from Section 4: structure your context with Goal-Context-Expectations, use meta-prompting to let AI help you refine your ask, and keep the conversation going.

Remember: You don't need to craft the perfect prompt yourself. Use the G-C-E structure from Section 4, then let Copilot help you improve it.

The Prompt Designer Meta-Prompt

This is your primary tool for turning a documented use case into a ready-to-use prompt.

Act as an Expert Prompt Designer. Based on the use case details provided, draft a comprehensive and clear prompt I can use to ask for your help with this use case. The prompt should briefly restate the context, describe the output I want you to produce, include my key constraints, and end by flagging any assumptions and requesting any missing information. After you propose the prompt, suggest 2-3 optional tweaks I could make to adapt it for different situations. Here are the details: [PASTE YOUR DOCUMENTED USE CASE FROM THINK STEP]
For Complex Use Cases: Problem Clarification

For tasks where the problem itself isn't clear yet:

Role: Act as a Problem-framing Partner. Task: Turn my documented use case into a clear problem statement and success criteria. Instructions: 1. Read my use case details below. Use only the information I provide. 2. If anything is missing or unclear, ask me up to 3 clarifying questions and wait for my answers. 3. Then respond with: * Concise Summary (50 words or fewer) * Problem Statement using this template: > "As a [role], I need help with [task / challenge] because [why it matters]. > Success means [outcome & any metrics]. > Key context: [contextual details, constraints, assumptions]." * What Success Looks Like: 3-5 clear, outcome-focused bullet points. Here are my use case details: [PASTE YOUR USE CASE]
Brainstorming with AI

Before jumping to a specific solution, use AI to explore your options:

Act as my AI Thinking Partner. Based on the use case details I share below, suggest several realistic ways Microsoft Copilot Chat could help me tackle this problem. Briefly explain the pros and cons of each option, recommend the 1-2 approaches that you think would work best in my context, and ask me any clarifying questions you need before confirming your recommendations. Here are the details: [Paste your problem statement, success criteria, and key constraints here]

Tip: Let Copilot draft, you steer. Your job is to validate accuracy, tighten scope, and adjust tone.

E EVALUATE — “Is this actually good enough?”

Input

Copilot's response to your prompt

Action

Challenge the output systematically and refine through conversation

Output

A refined, verified response you can trust

This is where critical thinking becomes visible. Don't just read the output and say “looks good.” Challenge it systematically.

After You Get Your Initial Response...

Remember, your initial response is rarely exactly what you want or expect. The true value of working with an LLM is in the conversation — each round of feedback gets you closer to what you actually need.

Review

Check relevance, completeness, and accuracy

Clarify & Expand

Ask for deeper explanations, examples, alternatives

Feedback

Tell AI what to keep, change, or remove

Refine & Iterate

Add context or adjust as needed

The “Improve This” Prompt

Compared to my goals and success criteria above, here is what works well: [list] and here is what is missing, unclear, off-topic, or incorrect: [list] Please produce a revised version that keeps the strengths and fixes the issues.

The EVALUATE Checklist

CheckQuestion
Accurate?Are the facts, figures, and claims correct? Could anything be hallucinated?
Complete?Does it cover everything you asked for? Is anything missing?
Relevant?Is it actually useful for your specific situation and audience?
Fit for purpose?Could you use this as-is, or does it need work?
What to Watch Out For
IssueWhat to Look For
Confident but wrongAI can state incorrect facts with complete confidence. Verify claims you'll rely on.
Sounds specific but is genericWatch for advice that sounds tailored but could apply to anyone. Push for specifics.
Agreeable but unchallengedAI tends to build on your assumptions even if they're flawed. Ask it to play devil's advocate.
Missing edge casesAI often gives the “happy path” answer. Ask “What could go wrong?”
Outdated informationAI training data has a cutoff. For time-sensitive topics, verify recency.
Tone mismatchThe output may be technically correct but wrong for your audience.
Evaluation Prompts

Use these meta-prompts to help you evaluate more effectively:

The “Fact Check” Prompt

Review your response above for factual accuracy. For each claim or statistic, indicate your confidence level (High / Medium / Low) and flag anything that should be independently verified before use.

The “Devil's Advocate” Prompt

Act as a critical reviewer. Challenge the response you just gave me. What are the weaknesses, gaps, or assumptions? What would someone who disagrees with this say? What am I missing?

The “Audience Check” Prompt

Review the output above from the perspective of [describe your audience]. Is the tone appropriate? Is it too technical or too simplistic? What would they find unclear or unconvincing?

If you can't explain why the output is good enough, you haven't evaluated it. Go back and check again.

O OWN — “Am I putting my name on this?”

Input

Your refined, evaluated output

Action

Make a conscious decision about what you're taking responsibility for

Output

A final output you can confidently use, share, or present

AI is not the author. You are. Before you use, send, or publish anything AI helped create, make a conscious decision about what you're taking responsibility for.

The OWN Checklist

CheckQuestion
What will I use?Which parts of this output am I keeping?
What needs editing?What do I need to change, add, or remove?
Are there any risks?Could anything cause problems if shared? (inaccuracy, confidentiality, tone, bias)
Who else needs to review?Does anyone else need to sign off before I use this?

Why This Step Matters

In an enterprise setting, you are accountable for what you share, send, or present, regardless of whether AI helped create it. The OWN step makes that accountability explicit. It's also the step that separates professionals who use AI well from those who just copy-paste.

The Ownership Test: Ask yourself: If someone questioned this output, could I explain and defend every part of it? If not, go back to EVALUATE.

Beyond the Loop

Know When NOT to Use AI

Highly confidential content

Why

If the data shouldn't leave your organisation, be cautious about what you paste into AI tools. Understand your organisation's AI usage policy.

High-stakes decisions

Click to flip
Why

AI can inform your thinking, but decisions with significant financial, legal, or safety consequences need human judgement and proper sign-off processes.

Tasks requiring real-time accuracy

Click to flip
Why

AI training data has a cutoff date. For current events, live data, or rapidly changing information, verify independently.

Emotional or sensitive communications

Click to flip
Why

AI can help you draft, but messages involving people's careers, wellbeing, or personal matters need genuine human empathy and care.

Do Not Stop Here: Extend the Value

Once you've owned the output, think bigger. What else can AI help you do with this work? Don't just finish the task; use this as a springboard for the next one.

Act as my AI Thinking Partner. Help me extend the value of this work by suggesting the most useful next steps (e.g. action plan, checklist, risk analysis) and how you can help me with these steps. Ask me clarification questions for additional context required.

When you can say “I reviewed this, I refined it, and I stand behind it,” you've completed TAE-O. And when you ask “What else can AI help me with?”, you've made it a habit.

Use AI to Learn AI

The best thing about AI is that you can use AI to help you get better at using AI.

This is one of the most powerful habits you can build. After every meaningful AI interaction, ask Copilot to coach you on how you did. It takes 30 seconds and compounds over time.

Act as my Prompting Coach. Looking at our full conversation above, review how I described my problem, how I provided context, and how I gave you feedback; tell me what I did well, suggest 3-5 specific improvements I can make next time, and summarize your advice as a short list of simple rules I can follow.

Make this a habit. Every time you have an important conversation with AI, end with this prompt. Your skills will improve faster than you expect.
